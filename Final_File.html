The Atomic Bomb

At 8:15 AM on August 6, 1945, the world's first atomic bomb destroyed Hiroshima. Code-named 'Little Boy', the bomb exploded 600 meters above the city center. The destruction was absolute and unprecedented.Buildings and trees within 2 km of the epicenter were burned down. Most houses within 3 km collapsed. The city became "a desert on the moon" with nothing left but rubble, ash, and twisted steel. This moment of devastation is the starting point of my Creative Journal capturing both the silence and the resilience that followed.

Nature's Remarkable Return

The First Flower

One testimony stood out to me: the belief that no tree or flower would grow from Hiroshima‚Äôs soil for 75 years. The words carry both hopelessness and the seed of defiance. My reflection draws on this: how nature, against expectation, became a companion in human healing. In my design, I illustrated plants rising from darkness to mirror fragile shoots piercing scorched soil. This symbolizes how survivors and nature both found ways to emerge after catastrophe. It is not only about biological survival, but about the renewal of spirit and the synchrony between human and natural healing.


In autumn 1945, just months after the bombing, a Canna indica bloomed within one kilometer of the epicenter. This single flower changed everything. When the human beings saw the tree growing back, they felt hope... The tree gave them the courage to live.

The 161 Survivor Trees

Within 2 km of the hypocenter, 161 trees survived. Today they are officially registered as hibakujumoku(A-bombed) trees. Each is marked with a plaque and lovingly cared for by citizens.

60 Years of Urban Forest Restoration

The trees that endured, the hibakujumoku, became powerful symbols of continuity. My website layers animation and testimony, echoing how memory itself is personal layered, ecological, and historical. Coding these growth sequences became a metaphor for regeneration: rebuilding line by line, much like Hiroshima itself.

Three Stages of Recovery

1945-1970: System-Making
First Greenery Campaign (1954): Mayors across Japan donated trees. Approximately 1,300 tall trees planted. Peace Memorial Park (12.21 ha) established. Initial survival rates very low‚Äîonly 4% in first attempts due to harsh conditions.

1975-1994: System-Working
Second Greenery Campaign (1975-1980): Citizens participated for first time through Greenery Promotion Committee. Mayor's 1957 tree donation campaign brought 7,000 large trees from neighboring communities with 55% survival rate.

1994-Present: Self-Management
Third Greenery Campaign (1996): Government-citizen collaboration flourished. Master Plan of Greenery (2001) finally recognized biodiversity conservation. Focus shifted to private greenery, rooftop gardens, and ecological networks.

Green Legacy Hiroshima Initiative

Founded in 2011 by Watanabe Tomoko and Nassrine Azimi, Green Legacy Hiroshima shares seeds and saplings from survivor trees worldwide creating living memorials to peace and resilience.

The Mission: A 1000-Year Project

Green Legacy shares the double message of caution and hope: recalling the dangers of nuclear weapons while celebrating the sacred character of life and the resilience of nature. Seeds and saplings grow in more than 35 countries in a sustained, long-term campaign for a nuclear-free and more sustainable planet. Descendants of Hiroshima's survivor trees now grow from Afghanistan to Chile, Singapore to the Netherlands, Lithuania to New Zealand. In Geneva, a robust Ginkgo biloba from Shukkeien Garden grows at the International Committee of the Red Cross. Former UN Secretary-General Ban Ki Moon planted another in Arianna Park.

Lessons for the Future

The bombing of Hiroshima delivered a sharp but brief burst of radiation, while later nuclear accidents such as Chernobyl in 1986 and Fukushima in 2011 left behind long-lasting contamination. This difference shaped the way environments and communities recovered. In Hiroshima, the first signs of plants reappeared within two months, and abnormalities in growth had largely disappeared after a few years. By contrast, the lands around Chernobyl remain unsafe decades later, while the destructive potential of modern nuclear weapons far surpasses past events.


The reflections from these comparisons highlight several key insights. Nature proved resilient in Hiroshima, but there are limits. Modern nuclear weapons would leave no chance for recovery. Survivors also spoke of a synchrony between their own healing and the return of plants and earthy materials, suggesting that human resilience is intertwined with natural regeneration. Community efforts played a vital role as well, with citizens donating trees and cultivating green spaces, which provided both physical renewal and psychological comfort. Finally, the city‚Äôs recovery was not quick: it took decades of consistent planning and action, showing that long-term commitment is essential for restoring balance after devastation.

The phenomenon of healing is wholistic and unitary, being experienced within the complex and constant biodance. Personal emergence generates through synchronization of one's own internal and external rhythmic patterns.
- Nagata et al. (2022)

Modern Peace Memorial Park with A-Bomb Dome

Today, Peace Memorial Park remains Hiroshima's spiritual center, the most visited site in Japan by foreigners, the most sacred place for Hibakusha and their descendants. The survivor trees stand throughout the city as living testaments to resilience.


The survivor trees of Hiroshima stand as living testament to nature's resilience and humanity's capacity for renewal. From the ashes of August 6, 1945, green shoots emerged. From broken trunks, new branches grew. From scorched earth, forests returned. These trees teach us that healing is possible, but only if we commit ourselves fully to peace, environmental stewardship, and the protection of all life on Earth.

Ultimately, my reflection is not a reproduction of history but an exploration of its echoes. By emphasizing resilience and ecological rebirth, I wanted the project to leave audiences with a sense of renewal that even in the darkest soil, life insists on returning. It is both a memory and a message: a call for peace, rooted in Hiroshima‚Äôs story.

May we never forget. May we always choose peace.
