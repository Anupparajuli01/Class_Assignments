Marya from Warsaw

Born Marya Salomee Sklodowska in Russian occupied Poland, she was forbidden to speak her own language. Polish culture was being systematically erased. Her mother and sister died when she was young, leaving her battling depression throughout her life. Despite poverty and oppression, education was valued in her family. She was a fighter from the start, teaching Polish secretly to peasant children‚Äîa crime punishable by exile to Siberia.

Early Obstacles: Russian occupation, forbidden native language, mother and sister's deaths, poverty, women banned from university, lifelong depression
"I feel everything very violently‚Ä¶ with a physical violence and then I give myself a shaking, the vigor of my nature conquers and it seems to me that I am coming out of a nightmare."
02
1883-1891

The Farm Tutor

Unable to study in Poland, Marya worked as a farm tutor, saving every coin to send her older sister Bronia to study medicine at the Sorbonne in Paris. For years, she sacrificed her own dreams. As an act of rebellion, she secretly taught Polish to peasant children risking exile. She studied advanced mathematics and science in isolation, teaching herself by candlelight, waiting for the day her sister would finish so she could finally pursue her own education.

The Sacrifice: Worked to fund sister's education first, secretly taught banned Polish language, self-taught advanced subjects in rural isolation, delayed her own dreams for years
"First principle: never to let oneself be beaten down by persons or events."
03
1891-1895

Marie at the Sorbonne

At age 24, Marya arrived in Paris, changed her name to Marie, and enrolled at the Sorbonne which was free but survival was not. Living in a freezing garret room in the Latin Quarter with no heat, she perfected her French while studying physics and mathematics. She was one of only 23 women among 2,000 students at the School of Sciences. In 1893, she became the first woman to earn a physics degree at the Sorbonne, followed by a second degree in mathematics in 1894.

Academic Excellence: First woman with physics degree at Sorbonne (1893), second degree in mathematics (1894), studied in poverty and isolation
"All my mind centered on my studies. I divided my time between courses, experimental work and study in the library. In the evening I worked in my room, sometimes very late into the night. All that I saw and learned was a new delight to me. It was like a new world opened to me, the world of science which I was at last permitted to know in all liberty."
04
1895-1903

The Scientist in the Glowing Lab

With her husband Pierre as her partner and equal, Marie began groundbreaking research in a converted shed. Working with 20kg batches of pitchblende by grinding, dissolving, filtering, precipitating, crystallizing over and over, she isolated polonium (named for her beloved Poland) and radium. The lab glowed with what she called "fairy light." She coined the term "radioactivity" and proved it was an atomic property. In 1902, after years of physically exhausting work, she finally isolated pure radium, determining its atomic weight as 225.93. Her meticulous measurements and new methods opened the door to atomic science.

Scientific Breakthroughs: Discovered polonium and radium, invented the word "radioactivity," proved it's an atomic property, created new methods to discover elements, pioneered atomic physics
"I am among those who think that science has great beauty. A scientist in his laboratory is not only a technician: he is also a child placed before natural phenomena which impress him like a fairy tale."
05
1903-1911

The Double Nobel Laureate

In 1903, Marie became the first woman to win a Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with Pierre and Henri Becquerel for discovering radioactivity). She balanced motherhood with her obsessive quest for knowledge, often in panic about her children, battling depression while making precise measurements no other scientist could achieve. After Pierre's tragic death in 1906, she became the first woman professor at the Sorbonne. In 1911, she won her second Nobel Prize in Chemistry for isolating polonium and radium becoming the first person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

Historic Firsts: First woman Nobel Prize winner (1903), first woman professor at Sorbonne, first and only person to win Nobels in two sciences (Physics 1903, Chemistry 1911)
"A great discovery does not issue from a scientist's brain ready made, like Minerva springing fully armed from Jupiter's head, it is the fruit of an accumulation of preliminary work."
06
1914-1934

The Radiant Legend

During WWI, Marie drove mobile X-ray machines "little Curies" to front lines with her daughter Irene, personally training operators and saving thousands of soldiers. She developed radiation therapy protocols still used for cancer treatment today. Despite knowing radiation was destroying her body (cataracts, suppurating fingers, constant illness), she never stopped. She denied to the end that her wounds came from the same source as her power. She was the first woman elected to the French Academy of Medicine, raised two brilliant daughters largely as a single mother, created laboratories and research standards, published detailed books ensuring her discoveries would benefit humanity. She died at 66 from radiation exposure, a famous woman whose work literally powers your computer today.

Enduring Impact: WWI hero saving thousands with X-rays, pioneered radiation therapy, created research protocols and laboratories, first woman in French Academy of Medicine, sacrificed her health for science
"I am one of those who think like Nobel, that humanity will draw more good than evil from new discoveries."

The Radium Queen's Legacy

From Marya Sklodowska, a Polish girl forbidden to speak her own language, to Marie Curie, the woman who coined "radioactivity" and opened the door to atomic science‚Äîher journey is not just about scientific genius. It's about obsessive dedication to understanding, about choosing the quest for knowledge even when it destroys you.


She knew the radiation was killing her. The cataracts, the cracked and suppurating skin on her fingertips until she could no longer hold a test tube. Yet she denied to the end that her wounds came from the same source as her power. Was she reckless? Self-destructive? Or was she simply a woman who understood that some discoveries require sacrifice, that imbalance might be the stuff of genius?


Her consistent scientific inquiry is what allows you to sit at your computer today. She created protocols, developed standards, built laboratories, published detailed books to share her work openly for humanitarian advances. She raised two brilliant daughters, battled lifelong depression, and broke every glass ceiling in her path‚Äînot because it was easy, but because she was driven by an unquenchable desire to know: Why? The question was the quest. The mystery was her true love affair.

Reflection










Watching Three Voices made me rethink how I see women‚Äôs resistance, especially when it happens in complicated social and religious settings. I have always admired Marie Curie, but the film showed me that the perseverance, clear purpose, and courage I connect with her are also present in the three Muslim women who lead the documentary. Their strength comes not from science, but from the hard work of speaking up for those who are not heard.

Each woman in Three Voices speaks from a distinct position. One draws from religious ethics, another from the law, and another from her responsibilities within a public institution. Their perspectives differ, yet they converge around a shared commitment to protecting women and reimagining what justice can look like in Indonesian society. What stood out most was the way they worked within the very structures that constrained them. Instead of rejecting tradition or authority, they opened new paths of interpretation and understanding. Their courage is thoughtful, patient, and grounded in the desire to bring about meaningful change.

This is where the parallel to Marie Curie becomes most meaningful. Curie worked in a scientific world that questioned her presence, yet she continued her pursuit of truth with unwavering resolve. The women in the film pursue a different kind of truth, one shaped by lived experience, ethical responsibility, and community well-being. Curie revealed the hidden world of atoms, while these women reveal the hidden burdens carried by those who suffer in silence. Both show that insight begins with the willingness to understand rather than to fear.

In the end, Three Voices is not a story about dramatic transformation, but about the quiet ways change begins. The film shows that even small shifts in conversation, interpretation, and awareness can alter the social landscape. This echoes Curie‚Äôs own steady and persistent approach to discovery. Both Curie and the women in the film remind me that progress often begins with a single determined step taken by someone who refuses to accept that the world must remain unchanged. This film remind me that a woman‚Äôs rise is not defined by ease or recognition. It is a quiet, persistent transformation that changes the world from within.
